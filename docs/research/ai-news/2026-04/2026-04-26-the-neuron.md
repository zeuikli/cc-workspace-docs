Well, the rest of the 50 pages then walk through where V4 actually won, which turns out to be a different race entirely: long-context economics.

And V4 serves all that long-form reading for about a tenth of what comparable models charge: $4 per million output tokens versus $14-15 for ChatGPT and Claude, per Hugging Face's [Tiezhen Wang](https://x.com/Xianbao_QIAN/status/2047520639364501596?utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut).

😿 Meta cut 8,000 jobs to fund its AI buildout.

Meta told staff this week it's cutting[ 10% of its workforce, roughly 8,000 people](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/meta-layoffs-8000-ai-job-cuts/?utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut), in what[ Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-23/meta-tells-staff-it-will-cut-10-of-jobs-in-push-for-efficiency?utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut) called a "push for efficiency" and[ CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/23/tech/meta-layoffs-10-percent-staff-ai?utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut) called an AI-driven reorg. The cuts hit hardest inside Meta Superintelligence Labs, the AI division Zuckerberg spun up last year to compete with OpenAI and Anthropic.

Here's what happened:

The cuts help fund Meta's $72B AI capex commitment for 2026, up from $47B in 2025.

Roles being cut include middle managers, researchers on non-agentic projects, and some of the AI hires Zuckerberg personally poached from Scale AI (the Scale deal alone cost Meta $14.3B).

Superintelligence Labs survives but is narrowing its focus to the Mythos-tier projects that actually ship.

Why this matters: This is the first "AI layoff wave at an AI-leading company" moment of the year, and it arrives right as Anthropic's Dario Amodei predicts white-collar cuts across the economy. If you work in tech, this is the quiet signal that the ZIRP-era org chart is finally getting restructured, not by the CFO but by the infrastructure build.

Our take: This is a Jeremy moment.

On Patrick O'Shaughnessy's podcast this week, SemiAnalysis' Dylan Patel[ told a story](https://www.youtube.com/live/LF3aUIM57uw?t=363s&utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut) about "Jeremy," an energy analyst on his team who spent $6,000 a day on Claude tokens for three weeks. Jeremy singlehandedly rebuilt a product that a 100-person data-services team at an incumbent had been working on for a decade.

Jeremy didn't get laid off. He got leveraged.

The Meta cuts are the inverse. When one person with the right AI tools can do the work of 10-to-15 people, the person most at risk isn't the one using the AI. It's the one whose job description overlaps with what AI now does by itself. The manager who shuffles Jiras. The researcher whose specialty is already solved. The middle-of-the-pyramid specialist whose output is now a 20-minute Codex run.

The[ three-part rule Patel keeps coming back to](https://www.youtube.com/live/LF3aUIM57uw?t=1828s&utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut): use more tokens, generate outsized value with them, and capture that value before the org chart gets rewritten around you. The alternative is being the team that got replaced by a Jeremy.

🎓 AI Skill of the Day: Let Claude grill you before it codes.

Matt Pocock of[ AI Hero](https://aihero.dev/?utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut) argued this week that "specs-to-code" workflows (write a spec, let AI generate code, never look at it) are vibe-coding in disguise and produce[ worse code each iteration](https://www.youtube.com/live/v4F1gFy-hqg?t=123s&utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut). His fix: make the AI interview you before it does anything.

The Grill Me skill (a few lines of instruction, now 13K+ stars on[ github.com/mattpocock/skills](https://github.com/mattpocock/skills?utm_source=www.theneurondaily.com&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=you-re-either-jeremy-or-you-re-cut)) flips the dynamic. Instead of you explaining the idea, Claude asks you 40-to-100 questions and walks every branch of the design tree. The goal is for you both to share what Frederick Brooks called a "design concept" (an actual mental model of the thing you're building). Pocock says it beats Claude Code's default plan mode for anything non-trivial.

Save this as a skill, or paste it at the top of your next planning chat:

Interview me relentlessly about every aspect of this plan until we reach a shared understanding. Walk down each branch of the design tree, resolving dependencies between decisions one by one. Ask about requirements, edge cases, user experience, data models, and failure modes. Do not write a plan document or code until I say we are aligned.

One favorite insight: this works even when you're not coding. Swap "plan" for "essay," "campaign," or "strategy," and Claude will drag a half-formed idea out of your head before it writes a single line.
